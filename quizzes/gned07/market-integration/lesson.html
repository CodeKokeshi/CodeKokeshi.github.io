Market Integration

Core definitions and key concepts. Read through before taking the quiz.

Core Definitions

Market

Derived from the word "mercatus" which means trading or place of transactions.

Integration

Is a state of affairs involving attempts to combine separate national economies into larger economic regions.

Market Integration

Refers to the process of creating a unified marketplace where goods, services, and capital can flow freely between countries and regions.

Trade Routes

Are established pathways, overland or maritime, connecting producers and consumers for commercial cargo transport, facilitating the exchange of goods, ideas, and cultures.

Monopoly

Is a market structure where one seller is the sole supplier of a good or service in the market.

The Great Depression

Is the greatest and longest economic recession in modern world history that ran between 1929 to 1941.

Tariff

Is a tax imposed by one country on the goods and services imported from another country to influence it, raise revenues, or protect competitive advantages.

Subsidiary

A company that is fully or partially owned and controlled by another company, known as the parent or holding company, operating as a separate legal entity.

Roman Period & Early Voyages

During the Roman Empire, the Silk Road allowed goods—especially silk, ideas, and culture—to travel between Europe and Asia. In the 15th century, explorers made intercontinental trade possible. Controlling powers: Spain, Portugal, Britain, and Italy.

Vasco da Gama

Discovered the first direct maritime trade route from Europe to India by sailing around Africa's Cape of Good Hope.

Christopher Columbus

Explored the Caribbean islands like Cuba, Hispaniola, and parts of Central and South America.

Ferdinand Magellan

His fleet became the first European expedition to cross the Pacific Ocean.

Napoleonic Wars & Beginning of WWI

Suez Canal

Built on November 17, 1869 by French diplomat Ferdinand de Lesseps. Shortened travel time between Europe and Asia.

Steam engines replaced sails and railroads, making transportation faster. Goods traded: spices, wheat, cotton, iron, and jute.

Economic structure followed the core-periphery pattern: core countries were the center of trading; periphery countries with abundant natural resources provided the raw materials and labor.

International Financial Institutions

IMF

International Monetary Fund

Organization of 189 countries. Ensures stability of the international monetary system.

World Bank

International Financial Institution

Provides loans and grants to governments of poorer countries. Comprises IBRD and IDA.

IBRD

International Bank for Reconstruction and Development

Lends to governments of middle-income and creditworthy low-income countries.

IDA

International Development Association

Provides interest-free loans—called credits—and grants to governments of the poorest countries.

IFC

International Finance Corporation

Largest global development institution focused exclusively on the private sector.

MIGA

Multilateral Investment Guarantee Agency

Created in 1988. Offers political risk insurance (guarantees) to investors and lenders.

ICSID

International Centre for Settlement of Investment Disputes

Provides international facilities for conciliation and arbitration of investment disputes.

Types of Market Integration

#1 Horizontal Integration

When a business combines forces with another company that offers similar products or services.

Merge — involves the combining force of two or more companies to form a new entity.

Acquisition — refers to one company purchasing another company, often with the goal of gaining control and reducing their competition.

#2 Vertical Integration

When the company expands either forward or backward by acquiring or merging with firms in an earlier or later phase of the value chain.

Backward Vertical Integration — ownership control of the products to a point earlier in the supply chain or the production process.

Forward Vertical Integration — ownership control of the products to a point later in the supply chain or the production process.

#3 Conglomerate Integration

Refers to the process or strategy of bringing different and unrelated businesses together under one management or corporate control. A conglomerate is a large corporation formed by several distinct, often unrelated, businesses operating in different industries, all under one parent company (holding company).

Importance of Market Integration

  • It is one of the ways to ascertain the extent of competitiveness in market.
  • It helps in the development of a single economic market at national level.
  • It provides better signals for optimal generation and consumption decisions.
