Core Definitions

Globalization

One of the most widely accepted explanations of globalization was given by Manfred Steger: "the expansion and intensification of social relations and consciousness across world-time and across world-space."

Globalization means

People, communities, and countries are becoming more connected, and these connections happen faster and reach farther across the world.

Globalization refers to

The actual processes that connect people, countries, and societies across the world.

Globalism

A belief or ideology. It is the idea, often promoted by powerful groups, that the global integration of economic markets is automatically good for everyone because it supposedly spreads freedom, democracy, and prosperity around the world.

Globalization is what is happening. Globalism is what some people believe about it.

Globalization is the emergence of a complex web of interconnectedness that means that our lives are increasingly shaped by events that occur, and decisions that are made, at a great distance from us.

Driving Force

Globalization proliferates because of its driving force which is technology progress.

One major disadvantage of globalization

Due to the intense exchanges of goods and services, people and the environment suffer.

Transnational corporations (TNCs)

Large companies that operate in multiple countries but manage their operations from a central headquarters. They produce goods, deliver services, and invest across borders.

Sovereignty

The supreme, independent authority a state holds over its own territory and people, meaning it has the ultimate power to govern itself without external interference.

Ideology

A form of social or political philosophy, or a system of ideas, that aspires both to explain the world and to change it.

Theory

A well-substantiated explanation or framework that helps us understand certain aspects of the natural or social world.

Four Characteristics of Globalization

  1. Globalization involves the creation of new connections and the strengthening of existing relationships among people and countries, even across political, cultural, and geographic boundaries.
  2. Globalization refers to the expansion and stretching of social relations, activities, and connections beyond local and national boundaries.
  3. Globalization involves the intensification and acceleration of social exchanges and activities.
  4. Globalization does not only happen on a material or physical level. It also happens on a subjective level, meaning it affects how people think, feel, and see the world.

Faces of Globalization

Economic Globalization Cultural Globalization Political Globalization

Economic: interconnectedness of economies and trade of products and other resources. Cultural: transmission and sharing of values, ideas, culture, and information. Political: high degree of political cooperation and political relationship from one state to another.

Theories to Globalization

1) The World-Systems Theory (Hyperglobalizationism)

Those who support this view are often called hyperglobalists. Proposed by Immanuel Wallerstein in 1974.

  • Global markets are more powerful than individual countries.
  • Nation-states are becoming less important.
  • Capitalism has spread across the entire world.
  • Core nations are the wealthiest and most economically developed countries.
  • Periphery areas often provide cheap labor and raw materials to the core nations.
  • Semi-periphery countries are in the middle between core and periphery nations.

2) The Regional Bloc Theory (Global Skepticism)

Regional blocs are groups of countries in the same geographic area that work together on economic, political, and security issues.

  • Association of Southeast Asian Nations (ASEAN)
  • European Union (EU)
  • Gulf Cooperation Council (GCC)
  • South Asian Association for Regional Cooperation (SAARC)

3) The Third Way Theory (Transformationalism)

The Third Way Theory promotes a practical approach to government that tries to balance the benefits and challenges of globalization.

  • Progressive taxation, investment in education and healthcare, and social safety nets.
  • International cooperation and regulation through global institutions.
  • Inclusive growth for poor and marginalized sectors.
  • Balancing global economic participation with national control.
