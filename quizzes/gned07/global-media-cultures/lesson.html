Media

Media

Media refers to the communication channels through which we disseminate news, music, movies, education, promotional messages, and other media.

Three Major Types of Media

Media can be classified into three major types: print media, broadcast media, and digital media.

Print Media

Print media refers to information that is physically printed on paper or other materials. Examples include books, newspapers, magazines, brochures, flyers, posters, journals, and newsletters. It is known for permanence because information can be stored, reread, and preserved for a long time, but it cannot provide real-time updates, production and distribution can be costly, and it requires physical access. Examples given in the lesson include daily newspapers, educational textbooks, comic books, and academic journals.

Broadcast Media

Broadcast media refers to media that transmits information electronically to a wide audience through radio waves or cable systems. It became prominent during the electronic age and is designed for mass communication. It can reach large audiences quickly and simultaneously. The main forms are radio and television. Radio delivers audio content like news, music, talk shows, and public service announcements, while television combines audio and visual elements for stronger storytelling and reporting. Examples include news programs, drama series, documentaries, advertisements, and live event coverage.

Digital Media

Digital media refers to content that is created, stored, and distributed using digital technology, especially through the internet and devices such as computers, smartphones, and tablets. Examples include websites, social media platforms, blogs, podcasts, online news portals, streaming services, and mobile applications like Facebook, Instagram, TikTok, and YouTube.

Evolution of Media

  1. Oral Communication is the earliest form of media. It existed long before writing systems and dates back to prehistoric times. Speech is believed to have developed at least 200,000 years ago, helping Homo sapiens distinguish themselves from other species. Because there were no written records, memory played a crucial role. Elders and storytellers served as living libraries, preserving history through repetition and performance. The Iliad and The Odyssey were originally transmitted orally before being written down.
  2. Script marks a major turning point because writing lets people record ideas, preserve knowledge, and communicate across time and distance. Early humans inscribed symbols on stone, clay tablets, and papyrus. Papyrus was an ancient writing material made from the cyperus papyrus plant and used by ancient Egyptians as early as 3000 BCE. One of the earliest writing systems was Cuneiform in Mesopotamia, while Egyptian hieroglyphics were carved on temple walls and monuments. These systems later evolved into alphabets, which made writing easier to learn.
  3. Printing Press revolutionized the production and distribution of information in the 15th century. Developed by Johannes Gutenberg, it enabled mass production of written materials. Before it, books were copied by hand and were rare and expensive. Printing made books, pamphlets, and newspapers widely available and helped increase literacy rates. The Gutenberg Bible is one of the most famous early printed works. Printed media also played a key role in shaping public opinion and political movements. In the Philippines, the first book published was the Doctrina Christiana en lengua espanola y tagala, printed in Manila in 1593.
  4. Electronic Media introduced new forms of communication powered by electricity, including the telegraph, radio, and television. These allowed information to be transmitted instantly over long distances. Radio became popular for news and entertainment, and Franklin D. Roosevelt used it for his famous Fireside Chats. Television combined audio and visual elements and made communication more engaging. A major example is the Apollo 11 moon landing in 1969, which millions watched in real time.
  5. Digital Media is the current stage in media evolution and is driven by computers, the internet, and mobile technologies. It allows instant, interactive, and multimedia communication on a global scale. In 2018, the Pew Research Center reported that 95% of adolescents ages 10 to 19 had smartphone access and half were almost constantly online. The most highly consumed platforms in that study were YouTube, Instagram, and Snapchat. Youth said they used social media for four reasons: communicating with friends, accessing information, entertainment, and self-expression. In the Philippines, newspaper consumption declined while about 96% of Filipinos watched TV for almost four hours. Increasing digital media consumption is linked to internet accessibility.

Media and Its Function

Marshall McLuhan

Media theorist Marshall McLuhan declared that "the medium is the message."

Meaning of the Medium Is the Message

McLuhan argues that the medium itself, meaning the form or channel of communication, has a greater impact than the actual content of the message. The way a message is delivered influences how it is understood, interpreted, and experienced. Different media platforms shape human perception, behavior, and even culture.

Example

Watching a news report on television is different from reading the same report in a newspaper. Even if the content is the same, the medium changes how people react to it. Because of this, media changes how we experience the world and influences how we think, feel, and interact with others.

Media and Globalization

The media have made globalization possible. Electronic media allowed opportunities to spread across the world. Media acts as the lifeblood of globalization by enabling the swift transmission of ideas, culture, and knowledge.

Culture

Culture can be defined as all the ways of life, including arts, beliefs, and institutions, of a population. Culture is what makes a country unique or sui generis because each country has different cultural activities and cultural beliefs.

Global Products vs Cultural Products

Global Products

Global products are goods and services that are standardized and distributed across multiple countries, transcending cultural and geographic boundaries.

Cultural Products

Cultural products are goods and services deeply rooted in the values, traditions, and practices of a particular culture.

Challenges of a Global Media Culture

Cultural Flow

Cultural flow refers to the movement and exchange of cultural elements such as ideas, values, traditions, language, fashion, music, and food across national boundaries.

1) Cultural Assimilation

Cultural assimilation happens when individuals or groups adopt the culture of a dominant or more powerful society, sometimes losing aspects of their original culture in the process.

2) Cultural Imperialism

Cultural imperialism refers to the dominance of one culture over others, often through media, business, or political influence. This can overshadow or weaken local cultures.

3) Cultural Hybridization

Cultural hybridization occurs when different cultures interact and blend, creating something new that contains elements from both or multiple cultures. Instead of one culture dominating another, they combine and evolve.

4) Glocalization

Glocalization refers to the adaptation of global ideas, products, or practices to fit local cultures and preferences. It combines "global" and "local." Instead of simply copying global trends, they are modified to suit local needs.

5) Cultural Differentialism

Cultural differentialism emphasizes the importance of preserving and maintaining the uniqueness of different cultures, even in a highly globalized world. It highlights that not all cultures will merge or become similar because some communities actively protect their identity and traditions.

Issues in Global Media Culture

Media Bias

Media bias happens when news or information is presented in a way that favors one side or opinion. Instead of being fully objective, some media sources highlight certain facts while ignoring others.

Fake News and Misinformation

Fake news refers to false information made to look like real news, while misinformation is wrong or misleading information, whether intentional or not.

Privacy Concerns

Privacy concerns arise when personal data is collected, tracked, or shared without full awareness or consent.

Digital Divide

The digital divide refers to the gap between people who have access to technology and the internet and those who do not.

Censorship

Censorship is the control or restriction of information by governments, organizations, or platforms.
