MeterSnap: A Justification of Development Methodology

Prepared for Proposal Defense

Abstract

This document outlines the selection and justification of the **Agile Scrum** methodology for the development of the MeterSnap mobile application. The project's unique characteristics, specifically the integration of high-uncertainty Machine Learning (ML) and Computer Vision (CV) components, necessitated a flexible and adaptive process. This paper argues that while traditional models like Waterfall or pure Incremental would have failed, Scrum provided the necessary framework to manage risk, pivot development priorities, and deliver a functional Minimum Viable Product (MVP) by decoupling the core application logic from the complex AI experimentation. The specific project events that validate this choice—namely the strategic pivot to a 'Manual Input' feature and the late-stage addition of an emergent 'Torch' feature—are analyzed as practical evidence of Scrum's efficacy.

1.0 Selection of Methodology: Agile Scrum

The MeterSnap project was developed using the **Agile Scrum** framework. Agile is a project management philosophy that prioritizes flexibility, collaboration, and adaptive planning in response to change, rather than strict adherence to an initial plan (Manzoor et al., 2024). Scrum is a specific, prescriptive *methodology* within the Agile philosophy that structures development into iterative cycles called "Sprints," as defined in its foundational guide (Schwaber & Sutherland, 2020).

We selected Scrum for two primary reasons:

  1. It manages high-uncertainty: The core of the project, a custom-trained CNN model for digit recognition, is a task of high technical uncertainty. Its success is not guaranteed and is dependent on data collection and experimentation.
  2. It delivers incremental value: Scrum forces the team to produce a "Potentially Shippable Product Increment" at the end of each Sprint. This ensured we would have a working application to demonstrate, even if the AI module was not yet complete.

2.0 Justification for Scrum in MeterSnap

The MeterSnap development cycle provides concrete evidence validating the choice of Scrum. The project's actual flow was not a failure to follow a plan, but a successful application of Agile principles.

2.1 The "Roadmap Pivot": Decoupling for Risk Mitigation

The most critical decision in this project was the pivot from the original, high-risk plan to a robust, phased approach.

  • Original Plan: [Camera → CNN Scanning → Bill Calculation]
  • Identified Risk: This plan creates a hard dependency. If the "CNN Scanning" (a research-heavy task) fails or is delayed, the *entire project* fails.
  • The Agile Pivot: Using Scrum, we re-prioritized the "Product Backlog." The "Manual Input" feature was identified as a high-value, low-risk story. It was pulled into an early Sprint.
  • Actual Plan: [Camera → Manual Input → Bill Calculation → (Future Sprints: Data Collection, Training, CNN Implementation)]

Justification: This pivot, enabled by Scrum, is the project's single greatest strength. It allowed us to decouple the application framework from the ML experimentation. We successfully built a stable, testable **Minimum Viable Product (MVP)**—a functional bill calculator—while isolating the high-risk AI component as a future, bolt-on enhancement. Recent MLOps research (Barros et al., 2024) strongly advocates for such "decoupled" approaches to prevent experimental AI from halting development.

2.2 The "Emergent Feature": Responding to Discovery

During a later Sprint focused on UI polish, an emergent requirement was discovered: the app would be unusable in dark environments (e.g., poorly lit utility closets). In a rigid model, this feature would be "out of scope."

In Scrum, this is a new "User Story" (`As a user, I want to read my meter in the dark...`). The "Torch" feature was identified as high-value and low-effort. The framework allowed us to add this story to the Sprint, integrate it, and release a better product. This is a classic example of "responding to change over following a plan."

3.0 Rejection of Alternative Methodologies

The selection of Scrum is further solidified by analyzing the guaranteed failure points of alternative models if applied to MeterSnap.

3.1 Why Not Pure Incremental? (The "Puzzle" Method)

A pure Incremental model is like building a puzzle. You finish one piece perfectly (e.g., the Camera module), then the next (the CNN module). This model *still* requires a fixed, upfront plan.

Point of Failure: We would have started the "CNN Module" (Increment 2) and become permanently stuck. We would have no product, no calculator, and no progress to show. The model cannot handle a pivot. It does not allow you to change the order of the puzzle pieces.

3.2 Why Not Pure Iterative? (The "Sketch" Method)

A pure Iterative model is like painting the Mona Lisa by sketching the whole painting, then adding base colors, then adding detail. You are refining the *entire* product at once.

Point of Failure: This model assumes the entire system exists from the start, just in a low-fidelity state. It would have forced us to build a "dummy" CNN in Iteration 1. We would be spending all our time refining a non-functional AI, rather than building the parts of the app that *did* work (Settings, Manual Input).

3.3 The Fusion: Why Scrum is the Correct Hybrid

Scrum is the correct hybrid of both. It is **Incremental** (we delivered finished modules like "Settings" and "Manual Input") and **Iterative** (we revisited modules to add polish and fix bugs). But critically, it adds the **Agile** layer of *adaptive planning* that allowed us to change the plan (the pivot) and add new features (the torch) based on real-time discoveries.

5.0 Anticipated Questions (Panelist Defense)

Q1: "Why isn't the AI (the core of your thesis) finished for a proposal defense?"

A: "We used Scrum to deliberately decouple the application framework from the ML experimentation. We prioritized the 'Manual Input' feature to create a stable, *testable* MVP. This ensures the app's core logic (billing, settings) is robust and provides immediate value. The AI is a high-risk enhancement scheduled for a subsequent sprint. This *mitigates the risk* of the entire project failing due to AI training delays, a common issue identified in MLOps research (Barros et al., 2024)."

Q2: "This just looks like 'Incremental' with a new name. What's the real difference?"

A: "The key difference is *adaptation*. A pure Incremental model follows a pre-set plan of increments. We *changed* our plan. Our initial roadmap was `[Camera -> CNN]`. When we identified the risk, we used the Scrum framework to re-prioritize our Product Backlog, pulling the 'Manual Input' story into Sprint 2. An incremental model would have forced us to get stuck on the CNN. Agile Scrum allowed us to *pivot* to deliver value first."

Q3: "You just added features 'whenever' (like the Torch). Isn't that just chaotic, unplanned coding?"

A: "That wasn't chaos; it was a response to an *emergent requirement*. During Sprint 3, testing revealed the app would fail in low-light. The Scrum framework allows for new 'User Stories' to be created. We identified the 'Torch' feature as high-value and low-effort, and the team had the autonomy to add it to the Sprint Backlog. This is a core strength of Agile—planned adaptation, not chaos."
