**Section: Media**

**Media** \- Media refers to the communication channels through which we disseminate news, music, movies, education, promotional messages, and other media. 

Media can be classified into three major types: **print media, broadcast media,** and **digital media.**

**Print media** refers to information that is physically printed on paper or other materials. Examples are books, newspapers, magazines, brochures, flyers, posters, journals, and newsletters.

Print media is known for its permanence, meaning the information can be stored, reread, and preserved for a long time. But it cannot provide real-time updates, production and distribution can be costly, and it requires physical access.

*Example: daily newspapers, educational textbooks, comic books, and academic journals*

**Broadcast media** refers to media that transmits information electronically to a wide audience through radio waves or cable systems. It became prominent during the electronic age and is designed for mass communication. Broadcast media is powerful because it can reach large audiences quickly and simultaneously

The main forms of broadcast media are radio and television. Radio delivers audio content such as news, music, talk shows, and public service announcements. Television combines audio and visual elements, making it more engaging and effective for storytelling and reporting.

*Example: news programs, drama series, documentaries, advertisements, and live event coverage*

**Digital media** refers to content that is created, stored, and distributed using digital technology, particularly through the internet and electronic devices such as computers, smartphones, and tablets.

*Example: websites, social media platforms, blogs, podcasts, online news portals, streaming services, and mobile applications. (Facebook, Instagram, TikTok, and YouTube.)*

**Section: Evolution of Media**

1. **Oral Communication** is the earliest form of media, existing long before the invention of writing systems. It dates back to prehistoric times, when early humans relied entirely on spoken language to communicate. Speech is believed to have developed at least 200,000 years ago, allowing Homo sapiens to distinguish themselves from other species.

Since there were **no written records, memory played a crucial role**. Elders and storytellers served as **“living libraries,”** preserving history through repetition and performance. Famous literary works such as **The Iliad** and **The Odyssey** were originally transmitted orally before being written down.

2. **Script** \- The invention of writing systems marked a major turning point in human communication. Writing allows people to record ideas, preserve knowledge, and communicate across time and distance.

Early humans began inscribing symbols on materials such as **stone, clay, tablets**, and **papyrus**.

**Papyrus** is an ancient writing material similar to thick paper, created from the pith of the cyperus papyrus plant, a reed found along the Nile River. Used by **ancient Egyptians** as early as 3000 BCE, it was made by layering, pressing, and drying strips of the plant's stem.

One of the earliest writing systems is **Cuneiform**, developed in Mesopotamia. Another example is Egyptian hieroglyphics, often carved on temple walls and monuments. Over time, these systems evolved into **alphabets**\- sets of symbols representing sounds- which made writing more accessible and easier to learn.

3. **Printing Press** \- The invention of the printing press in the 15th century revolutionized the way information was produced and distributed. Developed by **Johannes Gutenberg**, this technology enabled the mass production of written materials.

Before the printing press, books were copied by hand, making them rare and expensive. With printing, books, pamphlets, and newspapers became widely available, significantly increasing literacy rates. One of the most famous early printed works is the **Gutenberg Bible.**

Printed media also played a key role **in shaping public opinion and political movements**. 

In the Philippines, the **first book published in the Philippines** is the **Doctrina Christiana en lengua española y tagala**, a prayer book and catechism printed in **Manila in 1593\.**

4. **Electronic Media \-** The electronic age introduced new forms of communication powered by electricity. This period saw the rise of technologies such as the telegraph, radio, and television, which allowed information to be transmitted instantly over long distances.

Radio became a popular medium for news and entertainment, with leaders like **Franklin D. Roosevelt** used it for his famous **“Fireside Chats”** to connect with citizens.

Television further transformed media by combining audio and visual elements, making communication more engaging. A historic example is the broadcast of the landing on the moon of the **Apollo 11 in 1969,** which allowed millions of people worldwide to witness the event in real time.

5. **Digital Media** \- Digital media represents the current stage in the evolution of communication, driven by computers, the internet, and mobile technologies. It allows for instant, interactive, and multimedia communication on a global scale.

In 2018, Pew Research Center revealed that *95% of adolescents ages (10 to 19\) have smartphone access and half of these adolescents are almost constantly online.* The same study reveals that among the social media platforms: *Youtube, Instagram, and Snapchat* is highly consumed. The youth interviewed in this study revealed that they use social media for **four (4) various reasons: communicating with friends, accessing information, entertainment, and self-expression.**

In the Philippines, there was **a decline in the consumption of newspapers and approximately 96% of Filipinos watch TVs, devoting almost four hours to watching their favorite shows.** 

It is observed that increasing consumption of digital media is due to **internet accessibility.**

**Section: Media and Its Function**  
Media theorist **Marshall McLuhan** once declared that **“the medium is the message.”**

However, McLuhan argues that the **medium itself (the form or channel of communication) has a greater impact than the actual content of the message.**

According to him, the phrase means that **the way a message is delivered influences how the message is understood, interpreted, and experienced.** In other words, different media platforms shape human perception, behavior, and even culture

Watching a news report on television is different from reading it in a newspaper. For instance an influential person is giving a speech on video and broadcast vs. a newspaper report about it.

Even if the **content** is the same, the **medium** changes how people react to it.

Because of this, the **media changes how we experience the world.** It influences how we think, feel, and interact with others.

**“The media have made globalization possible.”** Electronic media allowed opportunities to spread all over the world. 

The media acts as the **lifeblood of globalization** by enabling the swift transmission of ideas, culture, and knowledge. 

**Culture** can be defined as all the ways of life including arts, beliefs, and institutions, of a population

Culture is what makes a country unique (**sui generis**) from each other because country has a  
different cultural activity and cultural belief.

**Section: Global Products vs Cultural Products**

***Global products*** **are goods and services that are standardized and distributed across multiple countries, transcending cultural and geographic boundaries.**

***Cultural products,*** **on the other hand, are goods and services deeply rooted in the values, traditions, and practices of a particular culture.**

**Section Challenges of a Global Media Culture**  
**Cultural flow** refers to the movement and exchange of cultural elements- such as ideas, values, traditions, language, fashion, music, and food- across national boundaries. 

1. **Cultural Assimilation** \- Cultural assimilation happens when individuals or groups ***adopt the culture of a dominant or more powerful society, sometimes losing aspects of their original culture in the process.***

2. **Cultural Imperialism** \- Cultural imperialism refers to the *d**ominance of one culture over others***, often through media, business, or political influence. This can result in the overshadowing or weakening of local cultures.  
     
3. **Cultural Hybridization \-** Cultural hybridization occurs ***when different cultures interact and blend,*** creating something new that contains elements from both or multiple cultures. Instead of one culture dominating another, they combine and evolve.  
     
4. **Glocalization** \- Glocalization refers to the **adaptation of global ideas, products, or practices to fit local cultures and preferences.** It **combines “global” and “local.”** Instead of simply copying global trends, they are modified to suit local need  
5. **Cultural Differentialism** \- Cultural differentialism **emphasizes the importance of preserving and maintaining the uniqueness of different cultures**, even in a highly globalized world. It highlights that not all cultures will merge or become similar; some communities actively protect their identity and traditions.

**Section: Issues in Global Media Culture**

**Media Bias \-** Media Bias happens when *news or information is presented in a way that favors one side or opinion.* Instead of being fully objective, some media sources highlight certain facts while ignoring others.

**Fake News and Misinformation** \- *Fake news refers to false information made to look like real news*, while *misinformation is wrong or misleading information*, whether intentional or not.

**Privacy Concerns** \- Privacy concerns arise when *personal data is collected, tracked, or shared without full awareness or consent.*

**Digital Divide** \- The digital divide refers to *the gap between people who have access to technology and the internet and those who do not.*

**Censorship** \- Censorship is *the control or restriction of information by governments, organizations, or platforms.*
